# Cram Sheet — Last Minute Review

## Formulas You Must Know

Let's run through the essential formulas. You need these cold on exam day.

Ohm's Law ties together voltage, current, and resistance. Voltage equals current times resistance — E equals I times R. Rearranged: current equals voltage divided by resistance, I equals E over R. And resistance equals voltage divided by current, R equals E over I. Know all three forms by heart.

For power, the fundamental formula is power equals voltage times current — P equals E times I. You can also calculate power as current squared times resistance — P equals I squared times R. Or power equals voltage squared divided by resistance — P equals E squared over R.

The wavelength formula converts between frequency and wavelength. Wavelength in meters equals 300 divided by frequency in megahertz. It's 300 divided by megahertz — not multiplied. That's a common trap on the exam.

## Decibels

Decibels are how we express power changes. Plus 3 decibels means double the power. Minus 3 decibels means half the power. Plus 10 decibels means 10 times the power. Minus 10 decibels means one-tenth the power. And plus 20 decibels means 100 times the power. These relationships are logarithmic, so they stack: 3 plus 3 equals 6 decibels, which is four times the power.

## Metric Prefixes

You need to know the metric prefixes and their values. Starting from the top: Giga means one billion, or 10 to the ninth. Mega means one million. Kilo means one thousand. Going the other direction: milli means one-thousandth, or 0.001. Micro means one-millionth, or 0.000001. And pico means one-trillionth, or 10 to the minus twelve.

## Band Privileges for Technicians

Know what bands you can use and what modes are allowed. On HF, Technicians have limited access. On 80 meters, 40 meters, and 15 meters, you're restricted to CW only at 200 watts peak envelope power. On 10 meters, you get two segments: 28.000 to 28.300 megahertz for CW, RTTY, and data; and 28.300 to 28.500 megahertz for CW and SSB. Both at 200 watts PEP.

On VHF and UHF, Technicians have full privileges with all modes at up to 1500 watts. This includes 6 meters from 50 to 54 megahertz, 2 meters from 144 to 148 megahertz, 1.25 meters from 222 to 225 megahertz, 70 centimeters from 420 to 450 megahertz, and 23 centimeters from 1240 to 1300 megahertz. The bottom line: VHF and UHF means full privileges, HF means limited access with mostly CW only.

## Key Frequencies

There are a few specific frequencies worth memorizing. The 2-meter FM national calling frequency is 146.520 megahertz. The 70-centimeter FM national calling frequency is 446.000 megahertz. The 6-meter FM national calling frequency is 52.525 megahertz. And watch out for 156.800 megahertz — that's marine channel 16 for emergencies, not an amateur frequency.

## Repeater Offsets

Each band has a standard repeater offset. On 2 meters, the offset is plus or minus 600 kilohertz. On 70 centimeters, it's plus or minus 5 megahertz. On 1.25 meters, it's minus 1.6 megahertz. And on 6 meters, it's minus 1 megahertz or minus 500 kilohertz.

## License Rules

Your license is valid for 10 years. After it expires, you have a 2-year grace period during which you can renew but cannot transmit. There is no minimum age for a ham license. Only the FCC can grant a license, and exams are administered by Volunteer Examiners, or VEs.

A control operator must be present at the station or operating by remote control. You must identify your station every 10 minutes during a contact and at the end of the contact. Identification must be by phone — meaning voice — or by CW.

The general power rule is to use the minimum power necessary for communication. On HF CW, Technicians are limited to 200 watts PEP. On VHF and UHF, the legal maximum is 1500 watts PEP.

Third-party traffic is allowed, but only with countries that have third-party agreements with the United States. Prohibited transmissions include music, obscenity, false signals, and encryption used to hide the meaning of communications. Encryption is only allowed for commanding a space station or radio-controlled model craft.

You can transmit without identifying your station only when controlling model craft. When operating another person's station, your privileges apply — not the station owner's.

## Propagation Modes

Line of sight is the normal propagation mode for VHF and UHF — your signal travels in a straight line, limited by the horizon. Sporadic E is an unpredictable phenomenon where patches of ionization in the E-layer suddenly allow VHF signals to travel long distances. Tropospheric ducting bends VHF and UHF signals through weather inversions in the lower atmosphere, sometimes for hundreds of miles. Meteor scatter provides brief VHF reflections off ionized trails left by meteors burning up in the atmosphere. And F-layer skip is the primary HF propagation mechanism where signals bounce off the F-layer of the ionosphere, working on 10 meters and lower frequencies.

## Safety Rules

Every station must perform an RF exposure evaluation. Maintain at least 10 feet of clearance between any antenna and power lines. For lightning protection, disconnect your feed lines when the station is not in use. Never climb a tower alone — always have a helper on the ground. Before working on equipment, turn off power and lock it out. And RF burns happen when you touch an antenna during transmission.

## Components

Here's a quick rundown of fundamental components. A resistor opposes current flow and is measured in ohms. A capacitor stores energy in an electric field and is measured in farads. An inductor stores energy in a magnetic field and is measured in henrys. A diode allows current to flow in only one direction. An LED is a diode that emits light. A transistor amplifies or switches signals. A fuse protects a circuit by breaking when current exceeds its rating. And a voltage regulator maintains a constant output voltage.

## Modulation Types

FM — frequency modulation — is the standard for most VHF and UHF voice communication. The frequency of the carrier varies with the audio signal. AM — amplitude modulation — varies the amplitude. It's used on airband and some HF communications. SSB — single sideband — is the efficient mode for HF voice. Use upper sideband above 10 megahertz and lower sideband below. CW, or Morse code, is the most efficient mode with the narrowest bandwidth. FT8 is a wildly popular digital weak-signal mode. And DMR — Digital Mobile Radio — provides digital voice on FM channels using time-division multiplexing.

## Common Exam Traps

Watch out for these traps on the exam. When you see "all these choices are correct" as an answer option, it's usually wrong unless it genuinely is all of them — read carefully. The wavelength formula is 300 divided by megahertz, not 300 times megahertz. Know all three forms of Ohm's law cold. Technicians can use 28.0 to 28.5 megahertz — not all of 10 meters. And always remember the FCC's general power rule: use the minimum power necessary for your communication.
